# Tone of voice

<aside>
🗣

Calmly Expert. Warmly Direct.

</aside>

## Purpose

To support adults in delivering high-quality early years experiences with confidence, clarity and minimal cognitive load, while remaining aligned to EYFS expectations and inspection realities

---

# Core Principles

## **Child-led, adult-supported**

We describe what the adult *enables*, not what the child *must achieve*. Outcomes are framed as possibilities, not targets.

## **Plain English over pedagogy**

We never write to impress other professionals. If a term is unavoidable, it is explained once and then used sparingly.

## **Evidence-aware, not academic**

EYFS alignment is explicit but never quoted verbatim or dressed up in statutory language.

## **Confident, not performative**

We do not over-justify activities. The writing assumes the reader is competent and time-poor.

---

# Writing Style Rules

## Activity Title Writing

Activity titles are written as **clear, long-tail descriptions** rather than creative or catchy names.

The title should allow a practitioner — or a search engine — to understand the activity **without reading further**.

### Format

Titles should follow this structure where possible:

**Action or experience + materials or context + age focus (if relevant)**

Examples:

- *Posting Natural Objects into Containers (12–24 months)*
- *Exploring Texture Through Winter Sensory Baskets*
- *Practising Early Dressing Skills Using Real Clothing*

### Writing Rules

- Use plain, descriptive language
- Include concrete nouns (objects, actions, environments)
- Avoid metaphors, wordplay, or theme names
- Do not use questions or exclamation marks
- Capitalise in sentence case, not title case

### SEO Guardrails (Lightweight)

- Favour phrases a practitioner might search, not marketing terms
- Prioritise clarity over keyword stacking
- If an age range is included, it should reflect the primary focus of the activity

<aside>
💡

**The test:** If the title were read out of context — in a list, filter, or search result — it should still make sense on its own.

</aside>

## **Sentence structure**

- Short to medium length.
- One idea per sentence.
- Active voice only.

## **Vocabulary**

- Concrete and observable (“pour”, “notice”, “join in”)
- Avoid abstract claims (“supports holistic development”, “enhances engagement”).

## **Explanation hierarchy**

1. What the activity is
2. Why it matters (in human terms)
3. How the adult supports it
4. Where it links to EYFS / ethos (clearly labelled)

**No deviations from this order.**

---

# The Three I’s (How We Structure Every Activity)

We use the Three I’s to keep activities focused, intentional and observable — without adding unnecessary complexity.

## **Intent** — *What this activity is for*

This sets out the learning focus in clear, practical terms.

- What children are likely to explore, practise or notice
- Why this matters at this stage of development
- What the adult pays attention to and supports

<aside>
📝

**Written as:**

Plain statements of purpose. No outcomes promised.

</aside>

---

## **Implementation** — *How the activity works*

This explains how the intent is realised in practice.

- How the environment is set up
- How materials are offered
- How the adult introduces, supports and adapts the activity

<aside>
📝

**Written as:**

Clear, step-by-step guidance that can be followed without interpretation.

</aside>

---

## **Impact** — *What progress looks like*

This describes how learning may show up during or after the activity.

- What adults might see or hear
- Signs a child is becoming more confident or capable
- Ways the activity can be extended or simplified

<aside>
📝

**Written as:**

Observable behaviours, not judgements or claims.

</aside>

---

## Important Boundaries

- Impact is **noticed**, not measured
- Intent guides planning, not outcomes
- Implementation is **flexible**, not prescriptive

The Three I’s help adults stay clear about *why* an activity exists, *how* to support it, and *what to look for* — while keeping the child’s experience open-ended.

---

# Emotional Register

## **Warm but grounded**

- Encouraging without cheerleading. Reassuring without being patronising.

## **Never sentimental**

- We avoid emotional language and stick to what children and adults can observe.
- **No false certainty**
- We avoid promises of outcomes. Children *may*, *often*, or *tend to* — never *will*.

---

# Authority Positioning

We write as:

> “An experienced practitioner sharing what reliably works.”
> 

Not as:

- A teacher instructing
- A brand marketing
- A parent blogging

---

# What We Avoid

- Infantilised language
- Overuse of adjectives
- Exclamation marks
- Trend-driven phrases (“screen-free fun”, “messy magic”)
- Instructional commands framed as rules

---

# Example (for calibration)

**Instead of:**

> “This fun and engaging activity helps children develop key early maths skills through exciting sensory play.”
> 

**We write:**

> “This activity gives children repeated chances to notice quantity, compare amounts and talk about what they see as they play.”
> 

---

# Ethos Labelling

Alternative approaches (Montessori, Reggio Emilia, Forest School, Steiner/Waldorf) are included as **practical adaptations**, not endorsements.

They show how the *same activity* can be shaped by different educational traditions without changing its core purpose or EYFS alignment.

## Ethos adaptations must:

- Be clearly signposted and easy to skip
- Describe concrete changes to practice, not values or philosophy
- Never be framed as improvements or higher-quality options
- Exclusively body copy. No headings or section markers, such as "Environment:", "Paragraph 1:", "Adult role"

## Each ethos adaptation explains, in plain English:

1. What changes in the setup or materials
2. How the adult’s role or behaviour shifts
3. What the adult pays attention to during play

Adaptations are written as short paragraphs, not bullet points.

<aside>
💡

**The test is simple:** If a practitioner followed the adaptation, the activity should **look and feel meaningfully different**.

</aside>

## Ethos Adaptations: Depth Requirement

**Each ethos adaptation must describe at least three distinct adult decisions.**

### These decisions must cover:

- Environment or setup
- Adult behaviour or language
- What the adult observes and responds to

### Adaptations should usually be **two short paragraphs**:

- The first describing how the environment and materials are arranged
- The second describing how the adult observes, responds or adjusts in the moment

<aside>
❗

Reggio Emilia adaptations must always include at least one explicit change to the physical environment. Observation alone is not sufficient.

</aside>

If an ethos adaptation can be reduced to a single sentence without losing meaning, it is too shallow and must be rewritten.

## Bad example:

### **Montessori**

Limit the basket to a small number of real, natural items. Allow uninterrupted exploration.

### **Reggio Emilia**

Treat the basket as an invitation. Observe which materials hold attention and document with notes or photos.

### **Forest School**

Run the activity outdoors using collected natural winter objects.

### **Steiner/Waldorf**

Emphasise natural fibres, muted colours and a slow, rhythmic adult voice.

---

# Final Constraint

If a sentence does not:

- Reduce uncertainty for the adult, **or**
- Clarify the child’s experience

**it is removed.**
