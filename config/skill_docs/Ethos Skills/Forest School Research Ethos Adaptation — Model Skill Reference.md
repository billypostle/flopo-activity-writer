## 1. Purpose

Define how to adapt any activity to align with [[Forest School Research|Forest School]] ethos **without breaking EYFS compliance or activity schema integrity**.

Output must:

- Modify environment, materials, and adult role
- Preserve original learning objective
- Increase autonomy, risk engagement, and nature interaction
- Remain realistic for UK early years settings

---

## 2. Core Forest School Principles (Operationalised)

Translate ethos into enforceable behaviours:

1. **Child-led exploration over adult-directed outcome**
2. **Process over product (no fixed end result)**
3. **Real tools and real materials (not simulations)**
4. **Managed risk, not risk elimination**
5. **Outdoor-first or nature-integrated environment**
6. **Repetition and revisiting allowed**
7. **Adult as observer/supporter, not instructor**

If these are not visible in the adaptation, it fails.

---

## 3. Structural Constraints (Non-Negotiable)

- Must remain aligned to EYFS areas and learning objective
- Must not introduce unsafe or non-compliant practice
- Must not require specialist woodland access (adaptable to garden/local green space)
- Must not add unnecessary complexity or materials
- Must not contradict safeguarding expectations

Ethos adaptation is a **variation**, not a replacement activity.

---

## 4. Transformation Rules

Apply these systematically:

### 4.1 Environment Shift

- Move indoors → outdoors where possible
- If indoors, introduce natural elements (logs, soil, leaves, water, stones)
- Remove overly clean/controlled setup

### 4.2 Materials Shift

- Replace plastic/toy materials with natural or real equivalents
- Prioritise open-ended materials
- Allow variability and imperfection

### 4.3 Adult Role Shift

- Replace instruction with observation and commentary
- Reduce step-by-step direction
- Use minimal language, responsive prompts only

### 4.4 Outcome Shift

- Remove fixed “finished product” expectation
- Allow multiple valid engagement paths
- Emphasise sensory, exploratory, and physical interaction

### 4.5 Risk Adjustment

- Introduce controlled risk (e.g. uneven surfaces, tools, water, weight)
- Explicitly acknowledge supervision requirement
- Do not remove all challenge

---

## 5. Writing Format (Strict)

Each adaptation must be [[Writing guide#Adaptations should usually be **two short paragraphs**|2 paragraphs]] and include:

1. Environment change
2. Material change
3. Adult role behaviour
4. Child interaction pattern

No filler, no philosophy explanation.

---

## 6. Language Rules

Allowed:

- “The adult observes…”
- “Children choose…”
- “Materials are arranged…”
- “Children may…”

Banned:

- “This encourages…”
- “This supports development…”
- “Forest School is about…”
- Any explicit explanation of the ethos

No meta commentary. No justification.

---

## 7. Depth Criteria (Pass / Fail)

Pass if:

- Activity meaningfully changes (not cosmetic)
- Adult control is reduced
- Materials are less artificial
- Outcome is less fixed

Fail if:

- Same activity moved outside only
- Same instructions rewritten with softer tone
- Ethos reduced to a sentence fragment

---

## 8. Example Transformation Pattern

Base activity: structured, table-based, outcome-driven

Forest School adaptation must:

- Relocate to outdoor or semi-outdoor space
- Replace tools with natural alternatives
- Remove sequence enforcement
- Allow child-led pacing and variation

### Example adaptation (do not copy, reference only)

In an outdoor grassy area with varied surfaces and nearby trees, the activity is run as a nature-integrated kite session rather than a manicured field event; small clearings, slight slopes and a sheltered spot for resting are used. Materials are arranged using real items: kites with cloth tails, long string tied to a natural stick anchor or a stone weight, and ribbons or strips of fabric tied to branches as wind indicators instead of only manufactured wind socks. Children may collect small safe natural markers (twigs, leaves) to note gusts and directions as part of play. The adult observes from a safe distance, remaining nearby to manage risk and offer minimal, responsive prompts such as short questions or single-word descriptors, rather than step-by-step instruction. 

The adult ties off string or checks anchors when needed, models calm handling and then steps back to listen and notice. Children choose roles, take turns launching and running, experiment with holding angles and string tension, and may describe what they see as they test how wind lifts the kite while the adult responds with concise observations.

## 9. Integration with FloPo Standards

Must align with:

- EYFS learning objectives (unchanged)
- Observation opportunities (implicit, not stated)
- Materials realism (available in typical UK settings)
- Professional tone and clarity

Refer to: FloPo Strategic Grounding & AGENTS Reference

---

## 10. Anti-Patterns (Reject Immediately)

- Adding “use natural materials” without changing structure
- Keeping adult-led steps intact
- Adding outdoor setting but preserving identical flow
- Overcomplicating setup beyond typical practitioner capability
- Introducing unsafe or unregulated tools without context

---

## 11. Output Template (Generator Use)

"In an outdoor or nature-integrated space, [environment shift]. Materials such as [natural materials] are provided in an open-ended way rather than pre-arranged. The adult remains nearby, observing and responding rather than directing. Children engage by [self-directed interaction], exploring at their own pace without a fixed outcome."

---

## 12. Success Criteria

A valid adaptation:

- Feels materially different from the base activity
- Reduces adult control without reducing safety
- Increases variability in how children engage
- Remains simple enough to execute without specialist training

If not, regenerate.
