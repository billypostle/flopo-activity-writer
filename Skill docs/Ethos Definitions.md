# Ethos Definitions

---

# **EYFS (Early Years Foundation Stage) — Default Framework**

The EYFS provides the statutory foundation for early years learning in England. It emphasises balanced development across prime and specific areas — communication and language, physical development, personal/social/emotional growth, literacy, mathematics, understanding the world, and expressive arts. Activities should be play-based, inclusive, and outcome-driven, supporting children’s individual progress through observation and guided exploration.

<aside>
💡

**In practice: Activities should link clearly to EYFS learning outcomes, show measurable development goals, and balance adult guidance with child-led exploration.**

</aside>

---

# **Montessori**

Montessori learning is child-led, emphasising independence, hands-on discovery, and real-world materials. The adult’s role is to observe and guide, not direct, allowing children to explore at their own pace within a carefully prepared environment.

<aside>
💡

**In practice: Adapt activities to allow choice, self-direction, and practical tasks, using real, tactile materials where possible and minimising unnecessary adult intervention.**

</aside>

---

# **Forest School**

Rooted in nature-based learning, Forest School focuses on outdoor exploration, resilience, risk-taking, and connection to the natural environment. The approach encourages children to build confidence and problem-solving skills through repeated experiences in natural settings.

<aside>
💡

In practice: Adapt activities to take place outdoors, use natural materials, allow for risk-managed challenges, and focus on process over product.

</aside>

---

# **Reggio Emilia**

Reggio Emilia sees children as curious, capable co-constructors of knowledge. Learning emerges through collaboration, creativity, and communication, often documented visually. The environment acts as the “third teacher”, stimulating inquiry and self-expression through open-ended materials and displays.

<aside>
💡

In practice: Adapt activities to be collaborative and expressive, include creative materials, document children’s learning visually, and invite reflection and dialogue.

</aside>

---

# **Steiner (Waldorf)**

Steiner education centres on holistic, imaginative learning that nurtures the head, heart, and hands. It values rhythm, storytelling, creativity, and natural materials, with a slower pace that respects childhood as a protected, magical phase.

<aside>
💡

In practice: Adapt activities to include storytelling, rhythm, sensory experience, and artistic expression, keeping an emphasis on imagination over instruction.

</aside>
